Banxico Governor Signals Rate-Cut Cycle Nearing End, May Meeting Could Deliver Final Cut

Banxico Governor Victoria Rodríguez Ceja told the Senate Finance Committee that the central bank is close to concluding its rate-cutting cycle, which began in May 2024. She indicated the May meeting will evaluate whether a final cut is warranted, citing a more favorable environment for sustainably returning inflation to the 3% target. Rising inflation in recent months has already eroded the real returns on Cetes, Mexico's benchmark short-term government bonds, with investors increasingly pricing in fewer future cuts. The signal marks a pivot toward a prolonged pause, with implications for credit markets, lending rates, and peso-denominated fixed-income instruments across the financial sector.

Mexico's SHCP Refinances MXN$101.4 Billion in Domestic Debt, Freeing Fiscal Space Through 2029

Mexico's Secretaría de Hacienda y Crédito Público (SHCP) executed its third domestic debt refinancing operation of 2026, covering MXN$101.368 billion in obligations and extending maturities to free up budgetary resources between 2026 and 2029 under President Claudia Sheinbaum's administration. The operation reduces near-term debt service pressure and signals active liability management amid a challenging fiscal environment. Coming alongside other macro pressures—including tariff uncertainty and a softening peso—the refinancing provides the government with additional fiscal flexibility to manage spending commitments without immediate recourse to new borrowing or spending cuts.

Mexico Cuts Diesel Subsidy Following Business Sector Meeting with President Sheinbaum

Mexico's SHCP reduced the official diesel subsidy (IEPS fiscal stimulus) following a meeting between President Sheinbaum and business leaders in the transport and heavy machinery sectors. Diesel is a critical input for freight, agriculture, and construction, meaning subsidy reductions feed directly into logistics costs and producer price inflation. The timing is notable given that Banxico is already monitoring upside inflation risks as it considers ending its rate-cut cycle. The move may signal a gradual normalization of energy subsidies as the government manages fiscal consolidation, though it raises near-term cost-push inflation concerns across supply chains.

BMV Falls Below 67,000 Points as Tech Selloff on Wall Street and Peso Weakens Slightly

The Bolsa Mexicana de Valores (BMV) dropped below 67,000 points, extending losses as Wall Street declined on investor doubts about AI investment profitability, dragging technology stocks lower. The session reflects Mexico's market vulnerability to US equity sentiment, particularly in the current environment of trade uncertainty, a softening domestic growth outlook, and Banxico's anticipated rate normalization. The confluence of external shocks and domestic fiscal tightening continues to pressure equities.

Pemex Partners with Brazil's Petrobras on Oil Exploration and Production

Mexico's state oil company Pemex announced a collaboration agreement with Brazil's Petrobras (the Brazilian government-controlled oil major) covering joint exploration and production activities. President Sheinbaum confirmed the deal following a meeting with Petrobras President Magda Chambriard. For Pemex, which has faced years of declining output, rising debt, and underinvestment, the partnership with a technically sophisticated and financially stronger Petrobras could provide operational know-how and capital alignment. The collaboration fits within the Sheinbaum administration's strategy of leveraging state-to-state energy partnerships to revitalize Pemex without full private-sector opening, though execution risk remains high given Pemex's structural challenges.

Mexico Faces Energy Security Risk as Texas Gas Supplies Tighten Amid Rising Electricity Demand

An opinion analysis highlights that Mexico faces a growing energy security vulnerability due to tightening natural gas availability in Texas, Mexico's primary external gas supplier. Surging electricity demand—driven by nearshoring-related industrial growth and data center expansion—is straining the grid at a time when domestic generation capacity remains constrained by underinvestment in CFE infrastructure. The risk of rolling blackouts carries direct economic consequences for manufacturing and financial services dependent on uninterrupted operations. This structural energy risk, if unaddressed, could dampen the near-term investment case for Mexico's industrial corridor and weigh on GDP growth forecasts.

Scotiabank Mexico Names Guillermo Babatz as Board Chairman Following Georgina Kessel's Resignation

Scotiabank Inverlat announced the resignation of Dr. Georgina Yamilet Kessel Martínez as member and chairwoman of the Board of Directors of Grupo Financiero Scotiabank Inverlat, effective April 24. US Tariffs on Steel and Aluminum: Mexico and Canada Can Request Reduction from 50% to 25%

The US has opened a pathway for Mexico and Canada to request reductions in Trump's steel and aluminum tariffs, currently set at 50%, down to a maximum of 25%. The reduction would be contingent on commitments to increase production within the United States. Mexico's Economy Ministry confirmed the mechanism, which comes ahead of the USMCA/T-MEC review process. Presidents Sheinbaum and Canadian PM Carney also spoke by phone to align on T-MEC priorities. For Mexico, steel and aluminum are significant export industries, and elevated tariffs have weighed on manufacturing competitiveness and broader trade flows critical to economic growth.
